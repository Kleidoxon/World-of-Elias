ELIAS — Draft V0.1
World Bible & Province Atlas
A living document. Subject to revision with each draft.


CRAFT NOTE — The World's Name
Naming a world "Elias" is a quiet but powerful choice. It sounds human — almost intimate,
almost biographical — rather than grand and invented. This creates an immediate narrative
question: who was Elias? A founding god? A dead mage? A sleeping king? Leaving that answer
unresolved until the right moment is a significant storytelling asset. Recommendation: Elias
was the First Weaver — a mage-god who shaped the world from raw chaos and then vanished,
either dying, ascending, or simply walking away. The world carries his name like a gravestone.
Every province interprets his legacy differently. That disagreement is itself political.


I. COSMOLOGY & MAGIC FOUNDATION
The Weave
Magic in Elias is not a force — it is a fabric. The world was woven into existence by Elias
the First Weaver, and the threads of that weaving (called the Weave) still run through all
living things, through stone and soil, tide and flame. Every mage is someone who can feel the
Weave and pull its threads.
Source: The Weave — omnipresent but unevenly distributed. Concentrated along ley lines; thinnest
in deserts and scarred wastelands; completely severed in the Corruption Zones of Duskmore.
Cost: Pulling at the Weave pulls back. Minor magic costs focus and stamina. Major magic costs
years of life, sanity, or memory. Catastrophic magic (reshaping terrain, raising the dead, altering
weather for days) leaves the mage permanently diminished — or dead.
Limits:

Magic cannot unmake death. It can delay it, simulate it, and communicate with the dead — but
never reverse it. This is the First Law, and every mage knows it.
Magic cannot compel genuine love or loyalty — only the appearance of it.
Magic does not function reliably near Black-fire Stone in its raw, unrefined state (a key
geopolitical fact; see Solrath).
Bullets cannot be stopped by a mage ward unless the ward was established before the trigger
was pulled. Combat mages must predict, not react.

Visibility: Active Weave-work glows faintly — the colour depends on the school of magic and
the mage's bloodline. Imperial mages tend toward silver-white. Aelindra scholars glow violet.
Vethara forest-binders, deep green. Solrath fire-workers, a harsh amber-orange.
Rarity: Roughly 1 in 40 people is Weave-sensitive. Of those, perhaps 1 in 10 has enough
sensitivity to be trained. Full mages — those who can reshape the world — are perhaps 1 in 1,000.

CRAFT NOTE — Guns & Magic
The coexistence of firearms and magic is your most interesting mechanical tension. Establish
the rule early and hold to it: magic is powerful but not instant. A mage who needs three
seconds to raise a ward dies to a soldier who needs one second to pull a trigger. This makes
conventional soldiers genuinely dangerous to mages — and drives the political fear that mages
have of imperial gun battalions. It also makes Thurak's military doctrine interesting: they
train riflemen specifically to break mage concentration. The moment a bullet clips a mage mid-
cast, the Weave thread they're holding snaps back. What happens then is never pretty.


II. THE POLITICAL LANDSCAPE
The Five Factions
Elias is governed — loosely, fractiously — by the Imperial Throne in Valdris. In theory,
the Throne rules all eleven provinces through a system of appointed Governors and elected
Provincial Councils. In practice, five power blocs have made that theory increasingly fictional.

1. THE IMPERIAL THRONE (Valdris — Centre)
"The Weave holds all threads. So too the Throne."
The oldest institution in Elias. The current Emperor is Cassian IV — intelligent, ageing,
and increasingly surrounded by advisors whose interests diverge sharply from his own. The
Throne controls the largest single military force, the imperial tax apparatus, and the licensing
of all commercial magic use. Its weakness: it is over-extended. Eleven provinces are too many
for one centre to hold when four of them are pulling outward simultaneously.

CRAFT NOTE: The most dramatically interesting emperors are not evil — they are tired and
right about the wrong things. Cassian IV should believe, genuinely, that the Throne is the
only thing standing between Elias and civil war. He is partially correct. That partial
correctness makes him dangerous and tragic simultaneously.


2. THE MERCHANT COMPACT (Seranthos + Lyrannis + Keldrim)
"Coin flows where walls do not."
An economic alliance, not an ideological one. Three provinces whose prosperity depends on
trade routes that the imperial tariff system actively hinders. The Compact does not want to
destroy the empire — it wants to renegotiate the terms of membership. Their leverage: Keldrim
controls the only reliable mountain crossing between east and west Elias. Lyrannis controls the
sea lanes. Seranthos holds two-thirds of the empire's banking reserves. Together, they could
strangle the imperial economy without firing a single shot.

CRAFT NOTE: The Compact is the faction most readers will initially sympathise with —
reasonable people who want fair trade. This makes them ideal for a midpoint betrayal. Someone
within the Compact is willing to go much further than mere renegotiation. The question is who,
and what they're willing to sacrifice to get there.


3. THE MAGE COUNCIL OF AELINDRA (Aelindra — Northern Highlands)
"The Weave answers to no throne."
The oldest magical institution in Elias, predating the empire by four centuries. The Council
does not seek political power in the conventional sense — it seeks independence from political
power. Imperial law requires all mages to register, pay a licensing tithe, and submit to
inspection. The Council regards this as an obscenity. They have complied, nominally, for a
generation. The compliance is ending.
What drives their urgency: a discovery in the deep archives of Aelindra Spire. Something about
the true nature of the Weave — and the cost of what the empire is doing to Black-fire Stone
in Solrath — that the Council has not yet made public.

CRAFT NOTE: The Mage Council's secret is your most powerful narrative slow-burn. Don't
reveal what they found too early. Let it surface in fragments — a scholar who drinks too much
and says too much; a Spire library whose certain section is always locked; a mage who left
the Council under circumstances no one discusses. The reader should piece it together before
it's stated. When it's finally stated, it should recontextualise everything that came before.


4. THE EMBER COMPACT (Solrath + The Ashen Hand of Morvaine)
"What was taken by fire will be returned by fire."
The most radical faction. Born from two separate grievances that found each other: Solrath's
indigenous population, whose land sits atop Black-fire Stone deposits that the empire extracts
and profits from while Solrath itself starves; and Morvaine's industrial workers — The Ashen
Hand — who produce the empire's iron, its guns, its machinery, and cannot afford to eat the
food they see shipped past their doors to Valdris.
The Ember Compact does not want to renegotiate. It wants to burn the current structure to
ash and build something new on the scorched earth.

CRAFT NOTE: The Ember Compact needs a leader who is genuinely right about the injustice
and genuinely terrifying in their methods. The moral weight of the novel depends on not making
the revolutionaries cartoonishly evil. They are correct. They are also willing to do things
that the reader will find unconscionable. That gap is the story.


5. THE GRAY LEGION (Thurak — Southern Frontier)
"Victory is the only argument that does not require a translator."
General Varek Thurn has commanded Thurak's military for eleven years. He is the most
effective military mind in Elias. He is loyal to the empire — and he is watching the empire
slowly fail. His own loyalty is becoming a liability. The Gray Legion controls the best-equipped
conventional army in Elias: twenty thousand riflemen, six artillery regiments, and three
thousand cavalry. If Thurn decides to act rather than wait, the map changes overnight.

CRAFT NOTE: Thurn should not appear until he matters. His absence from early chapters
should be felt as weight — other characters reference him, fear him, seek to secure his
loyalty. When he finally appears on the page, the reader should already understand what it
means that this specific man is in this specific room.

PROVINCE 1 — VALDRIS
The Heartland | Imperial Capital Province
Economy: ◆◆◆◆◆ Opulent
"At the centre of all rivers, there is always a throne."
Geography: Rolling chalk hills, three major rivers converging at the capital, fertile land
that has never known famine. The landscape feels managed — every meadow deliberate, every
forest a planted one, every road wide enough for a military column.
Cities:
    City           Population    Character 
Valdris Atheum     ~380,000   Imperial capital. 
Political machinery, noble estates, the Great Archive, the Weave Licensing Bureau. Beautiful on the surface; rotten at the roots.

  City           Population    Character 
Archenmoor       ~60,000   Administrative hub. 
Where imperial paperwork is processed. A city of clerks, couriers, and quiet corruption.

  City       Population    Character 
Seldvane     ~40,000    The emperor's secondary residence. 
Quieter, older, full of retired generals and minor nobles nursing old grudges.
Political Character: The most loyal province to the Throne — because it is the Throne.
The nobles of Valdris have the most to lose if the empire fragments. Their loyalty is
self-interest wearing the coat of patriotism.
Military: The Imperial Guard (elite, over-equipped, chronically under-tested), and the
Throne's rifle battalions. The Guard's sidearms are the finest in Elias — precision-crafted
revolvers bearing the imperial seal.
Magic: All magic here is bureaucratic. Licensed, regulated, visible. Mages work for the
administration. Freelance magic is punished. The Weave still hums beneath Valdris Atheum, but
it has been domesticated.

CRAFT NOTE: Set at least one early scene in Valdris Atheum's underbelly — not the palaces,
but the clerks' district. The rot in the empire is most visible not in the throne room but in
the room where the throne's decrees are processed. A minor official who knows exactly what the
tariff numbers mean, and drinks to forget, is more revealing than any villain's monologue.


PROVINCE 2 — SERANTHOS
The Silver Coast | Maritime Trade Province
Economy: ◆◆◆◆◆ Opulent
"The sea does not ask permission."
Geography: Jagged western coastline, deep natural harbours, sea stacks, salt-white cliffs
visible for thirty miles on clear days. The wind here is constant — ships in Seranthos Port
barely need their engines.
Cities:
  City         Population      Character
Seranthos Port ~290,000      The trading city. 
Five hundred years of merchant wealth layered on itself. Banking houses taller than cathedrals. Ships from every province and beyond. The smell of salt, coal-smoke, and money.

 City       Population      Character
Galvernis    ~85,000        The banking city. 
Where the Merchant Compact's financiers live and work. Every major loan in Elias is denominated here.

 City         Population       Character
Whitecrow Bay  ~35,000       The smugglers' city  
Everyone knows it, nobody says it officially. What can't be shipped through the Port comes through Whitecrow.
Political Character: Charming, cosmopolitan, and absolutely ruthless beneath the charm.
The Merchant Compact was born in Galvernis. The Seranthos merchant houses fund both sides of
every political dispute, ensuring they profit regardless of outcome.
Military: A naval fleet of thirty-two warships (the pride of Elias), coastal artillery
batteries, and harbour-guard rifle units. The Seranthos Navy has not fought a battle in
forty years. This may be their greatest strategic weakness.
Magic: Sea-magic (Tidecalling) — a tradition unique to Seranthos. Tidecallers can sense
and manipulate weather at sea, communicate across long distances via resonant water channels,
and (in skilled hands) guide currents. The merchant houses have Tidecallers on retainer;
the navy has them embedded in every vessel.

CRAFT NOTE: Seranthos Port should feel like the novel's primary grey-zone — a city where
information, money, and allegiances all blur together. It is where characters from opposing
factions can plausibly meet, because everyone comes to Seranthos to trade something.
Consider making one scene here that appears to be about commerce and is actually about
something much more dangerous.


PROVINCE 3 — AELINDRA
The Arcane Highlands | Northern Mage Province
Economy: ◆◆◆◆◇ Prosperous
"What the eye cannot see, the Weave remembers."
Geography: A high plateau at the northern extreme of Elias, perpetually shrouded in violet-
tinged mist. The ley lines here run so dense they are visible at night — silver threads of
light crossing the sky like a second constellation. Outsiders find Aelindra unsettling.
Aelindra's residents find everywhere else unacceptably quiet.
Cities:
 City         Population Character 
Aelindra Spire ~95,000  The mage-city. 
The Spire itself is a tower of uncertain height — estimates vary because looking at it directly for too long tends to make people forget what they were counting. The Mage Council meets here. The deep archives are here. Whatever the Council found is here.Mirvath~30,000The scholarly outpost. Where mages who prefer research to politics retreat. Quiet, brilliant, collectively in denial about what the Council's discovery means.Coldenshall~18,000The border town. Where Aelindra trades with the outside world. A strange city — half mage apprentices, half traders who've learned not to ask questions about the glowing things they're shipping.
Political Character: Isolationist, proud, increasingly afraid. The Mage Council has known
for eight months what the Black-fire Stone extraction in Solrath is doing to the Weave's deep
structure. They have not told anyone. The weight of that secret is visible on the faces of
Council members who've been to the deep archives. Others pretend not to notice.
Military: Battle-mages. No conventional army. The Spire is warded beyond what any
conventional force could breach — but the Council knows that enough artillery, fired fast
enough, doesn't need to break the wards. It just needs to outlast them.
Magic: The highest concentration of the Weave in Elias. Aelindra mages can do things here
that are impossible elsewhere. They also know that this makes the Spire a target.

CRAFT NOTE: The Spire's library is where your protagonist (or a key character) will
eventually come — and what they find must be worse than they expected, but not in the way
they expected. The horror should be structural, not monstrous. Not a creature. An
understanding. Something that reframes every scene that came before it.


PROVINCE 4 — MORVAINE
The Iron Province | Industrial Heartland
Economy: ◆◆◆◇◇ Stable (official figure — true figure for workers: ◆◆◇◇◇ Struggling)
"Iron does not lie about its weight."
Geography: A flat eastern plateau, its natural beauty completely buried under a century of
industrial expansion. Mine shafts pepper the ground like bullet wounds. The sky above Morvaine
Deep is the colour of an old bruise from dawn to dusk.
Cities:
CityPopulationCharacterMorvaine Deep~210,000The industrial city. Foundries, gun factories, ironworks, coal processing. The official economy is booming. The workers who run it live in tenements that lack running water. The Ashen Hand recruits openly here, because no one in power is paying close enough attention.Ashgate~70,000The mine city. Everything Morvaine produces starts here. The life expectancy of a miner is 43 years. Everyone knows this. Nobody in Valdris considers it relevant.Ironhallow~45,000The company town. Entirely owned and operated by the Ironhallow Industrial Consortium, a noble-backed corporation. Workers are paid in company scrip — currency that only works in company stores.
Political Character: The surface is stable; beneath it, a fire has been burning for two
decades. The Ashen Hand — Morvaine's revolutionary workers' movement — has cells in every
factory district. They are not yet ready to act. They are acquiring guns, quietly, from the
same foundries that make the empire's weapons.
Military: A substantial imperial garrison, present specifically to suppress labour unrest.
The soldiers and the workers live in the same city, eat in the same taverns, and increasingly
find common ground when the officers aren't watching.
Magic: Actively suppressed. The empire banned magic in Morvaine's industrial zones after
the Foundry Incident of 843 — when a mage worker lost control mid-shift and destroyed a
factory block. The suppression is also strategic: a magic-free workforce is easier to control.

CRAFT NOTE: Morvaine is where your story's moral urgency lives. If your novel has a theme
about what political systems cost the people at the bottom, Morvaine is where that theme
becomes visceral. Don't describe the poverty abstractly. A child coughing in the coal dust.
A woman who works the night shift and hasn't seen her children awake in three weeks. The
specific cost of a system that works for everyone except the people running it.


PROVINCE 5 — CASSIVAR
The Breadbasket | Central Agricultural Province
Economy: ◆◆◆◇◇ Stable
"A hungry army is a defeated army. A hungry province is simply forgotten."
Geography: The great central plains of Elias — enormous, golden, and wind-scoured. The
horizon is always visible. There is nowhere to hide in Cassivar, which shapes its people:
practical, watchful, slow to trust.
Cities:
CityPopulationCharacterCassivar Fields~120,000The market city. Every grain auction in Elias sets its prices here. The city feels prosperous from a distance — wide streets, solid buildings — but the farmers who built it cannot afford to live in it.Goldenholt~55,000The river city. At the junction of two major waterways; grain barges pass through continuously. The dockworkers here have a saying: "We load the empire's stomach and our own stay empty."Mirren~28,000The quiet city. Agricultural research, seed vaults, water management engineering. The most technically sophisticated city most people have never heard of.
Political Character: Cassivar votes with whoever controls the water rights. For three
generations, that has been the Throne. If Morvaine's Ashen Hand ever extends its reach to
Cassivar's dockworkers, the calculus shifts.
Military: Provincial militia, lightly armed. One imperial cavalry regiment stationed here
to keep the grain routes open.
Magic: Agricultural folk magic — rain-calling (Waterweaving), soil-blessing, harvest rites
— is practiced openly in Cassivar and tolerated by the Throne because it keeps grain yields
high. These folk mages are not officially licensed. Officially, the Throne pretends not to
notice. Unofficially, the Weave Bureau tracks every one of them.

CRAFT NOTE: Cassivar should feel like the province that everyone takes for granted and
everyone needs. A scene here in the off-season — after harvest, before planting, when the
plains are bleak and the farmers have nothing to do but talk — could be the novel's most
quietly devastating. These are the people the political factions are fighting about and
fighting over while never actually fighting for.


PROVINCE 6 — THURAK
The Iron Veil | Southern Military Frontier
Economy: ◆◆◆◇◇ Stable
"The border does not move. Neither do we."
Geography: Harsh southern scrubland transitioning to true desert at its southern extreme.
Ancient battlefields — from three separate wars with the civilisations beyond the border — are
visible features of the landscape. Bones surface when it rains.
Cities:
CityPopulationCharacterThurak Keep~165,000The fortress city. Built to be besieged and never taken. The civilian population lives inside the military architecture — every district is also a defensive position, every tavern doubles as a magazine. A strange, disciplined city that never fully relaxes.Ironveil~60,000The weapons city. Gun foundries, ammunition works, blade smithies. The Gray Legion's equipment is made here. Quality control is religious.Ashborder~25,000The last city. The furthest south any province extends. Beyond Ashborder, the land belongs to whatever comes next — and what comes next does not respect borders.
Political Character: General Thurn commands more real loyalty here than the emperor does.
He has not yet asked the province to choose between them. When he does, the answer will
not be ambiguous.
Military: The Gray Legion — the finest conventional army in Elias. Twenty thousand
riflemen, six artillery regiments (the new breech-loading kind; the empire hasn't issued
these to anyone else yet), three thousand cavalry. Thurn also has, quietly, six trained
battle-mages integrated into his command staff. The Throne knows about three of them.
Magic: Combat magic as doctrine. Thurak's battle-mages are not scholars. They are soldiers
who can also do magic, and they train like soldiers, not like scholars.

CRAFT NOTE: Thurak is where your thriller elements live. A scene inside the Gray Legion
— officers arguing in a mess hall about what loyalty means when the institution you're loyal
to is failing — is a scene about the novel's central question. Thurn himself should be the
most human of the major power figures: no ideology, no vision, no cause. Just a man who
is very good at his job, watching the thing that gave his job meaning come apart.


PROVINCE 7 — SOLRATH
The Ashland | Southern Desert Province
Economy: ◆◆◇◇◇ Struggling
"What the empire calls a resource, we call our ancestor's bones."
Geography: Vast arid desert, dust storms that last for weeks, ancient ruins half-buried
in sand. Below the surface: the Black-fire Stone deposits — dense veins of a mineral that
amplifies the Weave catastrophically, burns hot enough to power engines, and is slowly,
irreversibly poisoning the deep ley lines when extracted at industrial scale.
Cities:
CityPopulationCharacterSolrath Dunes~85,000The resistance city. Where the Ember Compact was founded. The city has no love for Valdris — every family in it has a story of land seized, a relative arrested, a well poisoned by Black-fire runoff. The dust here is grey from the extraction sites.Miraveska~40,000The extraction camp turned city. Built by the empire to house extraction workers; now the most politically volatile settlement in Elias. Ember Compact recruiters do not need to recruit here — they simply point at the sky.Burnhollow~15,000The old city. Pre-imperial. The ruins of the civilisation that existed in Solrath before the empire arrived are partly intact here. The indigenous Solrathi come on pilgrimage. The empire has been trying to reclassify the ruins as imperial archaeological property for a decade.
Political Character: The Ember Compact is strongest here. Every Black-fire Stone shipment
north is a visible, daily reminder of what the empire does with Solrath's wealth. The
indigenous Solrathi people are not a minority in their own province — they are the majority,
they are organised, and they have been patient for longer than patience should require.
Military: No conventional army — the empire's garrison has driven all resistance underground.
Desert fighters, ambush tactics, improvised explosives, stolen weapons, and (in the deepest
desert, where the extraction runoff concentrates the Weave into something barely controllable)
raw fire magic that would make an Aelindra scholar's hair stand on end.
Magic: Elemental fire magic (Embercalling), learned without formal training, fuelled by
the ambient energy of Black-fire deposits. Powerful, volatile, and doing something to the
deep Weave that the Mage Council is very carefully not discussing.

CRAFT NOTE: Solrath is the novel's moral anchor — the place where the cost of the empire
is most undeniable, most physical, most unjust. The protagonists may have complicated feelings
about the Ember Compact's methods. They should have no complicated feelings about what is
being done to Solrath. If a reader can finish your novel and feel neutral about Solrath,
something in the writing has gone wrong.


PROVINCE 8 — KELDRIM
The Stonegate | Mountain Province
Economy: ◆◆◆◆◇ Prosperous
"Every road through the mountains goes through us. We have not forgotten this."
Geography: The Keldrim Range — a spine of grey-white peaks running north to south, dividing
eastern and western Elias. The only reliable mountain crossings are the three Stonegate passes,
all of which are owned, maintained, and taxed by the Stonegate Guild.
Cities:
CityPopulationCharacterKeldrim Peak~110,000The guild city. The Stonegate Guild's headquarters; a city built for commerce and defence simultaneously. Every building is also a fortification. The Guild Masters live better than most provincial governors.Stonehaven~50,000The gem city. Where Keldrim's legendary gem mines are processed and traded. The gems here are not only beautiful — certain deep-mined stones have Weave-resonant properties that make them invaluable for magical instrumentation.Coldvault~20,000The archive city. Keldrim has been keeping records longer than the empire has existed. Every toll ever paid, every passage ever made, every agreement ever reached in the passes — all of it is here. The Stonegate Guild's legal leverage is enormous because their documentation is impeccable.
Political Character: Keldrim's power is leverage, not force. The Stonegate Guild holds
the passes — which means every army that wants to move from east to west (or vice versa)
has to negotiate with them. They have sold passage to both the empire and its enemies,
sometimes simultaneously, which would be called treason if anyone could prove it.
Military: Mountain troops, tunnel fighters, precision marksmen who know every ridge and
cave in the range. The passes themselves are fortified with ancient earth-magic architecture —
walls that respond to the stone around them, self-repairing, resistant to artillery in ways
that physics alone cannot explain.
Magic: Earth and stone magic (Stonebinding) — passive, architectural, generational.
Keldrim's mages don't cast spells in the conventional sense; they speak to stone and stone
listens. The Guild has Stonebinders embedded in every pass fortification, maintaining wards
that have stood for four hundred years.

CRAFT NOTE: Keldrim is your political wildcard. In the endgame of any faction conflict,
who controls the passes determines who wins. Consider a scene where two faction representatives
both arrive at Keldrim Peak to negotiate passage rights — and the Guild Master calmly plays
them against each other in the same meeting room, over tea. The Stonegate Guild has been
doing this for centuries. They are very good at it.


PROVINCE 9 — VETHARA
The Deep Forest | Northwestern Ancient Forest
Economy: ◆◆◇◇◇ Struggling
"The forest does not forget what was done in it. Neither do we."
Geography: A primordial forest that predates Elias by geological ages — trees with trunks
wider than houses, rivers black with tannins, mist that never fully lifts. The forest has moods.
Travellers without guides are found wandering in circles three days later, confused about what
year it is.
Cities:
CityPopulationCharacterVethara Grove~70,000The contested city. Built by the empire on land the Vethara people have tended for ten thousand years. The empire calls it a provincial capital. The Vethara call it an occupation. Both are correct. The tension here is quiet and absolute.Mosswick~25,000The border settlement. Where the imperial world and the deep forest meet. A strange, porous city where Vethara people and imperial settlers trade, intermarry, argue, and occasionally vanish.Darkbranch~12,000The deep-forest settlement. Technically part of the province; functionally autonomous. The empire's authority here is theoretical. The forest's authority is not.
Political Character: The Vethara — the indigenous people of the forest — are not part of
any faction. They predate all factions. Imperial colonisation happened within living memory of
the eldest Vethara, and the wound has not healed because the wound has not stopped being
inflicted. The Ember Compact has reached out to the Vethara for an alliance. The Vethara are
listening. They have not answered.
Military: Forest guerrillas of terrifying effectiveness. The imperial garrison in Vethara
Grove has suffered the highest casualty rate per engagement of any posting in Elias, despite
never fighting a conventional battle. The forest fights back.
Magic: Animist — Forestbinding. The Vethara's relationship with the Weave is not
academic; it is ecological. They do not manipulate the Weave so much as are part of it.
A Vethara forestbinder can speak to the trees, know everything that moved through them,
and ask the forest to close its paths to enemies. The forest usually agrees.

CRAFT NOTE: Vethara is where your colonial history lives. Handle it with the care of Domain
15 (Sensitivity & Research Ethics). The Vethara should not be a monolith — they have internal
debates, generational divisions, people who collaborate with the empire and people who would
burn it down. The elder who remembers the first imperial soldiers arriving is a different
character from the young Vethara who has only ever known occupation. Both deserve to be heard.


PROVINCE 10 — LYRANNIS
The Saltborn Isles | Eastern Island Province
Economy: ◆◆◆◆◆ Opulent
"The sea is our wall, our road, and our oldest ancestor."
Geography: An archipelago of twelve islands off the eastern coast, connected by sea lanes
and (on the three inner islands) by a system of coral-cable bridges that sway in the wind and
have never once broken. The outer islands are volcanic; the inner islands are ancient, stable,
and rich.
Cities:
CityPopulationCharacterLyrannis Isle~175,000The island capital. The most physically beautiful city in Elias — built on three levels of a volcanic island, cascading down to a perfect harbour. Also the most profitable city in the Merchant Compact's alliance.Seaforth~60,000The trade hub. On the largest of the inner islands; receives ships from beyond Elias that no other province officially knows about. Foreign intelligence enters Elias through Seaforth.Glasswater~30,000The volcanic island city. Built on the caldera rim of the youngest volcano in the archipelago. The islanders here are considered slightly mad by the rest of Lyrannis, which has a high tolerance for eccentricity. The volcanic glass produced here is irreplaceable for certain forms of Weave-amplification.
Political Character: Lyrannis is the most likely province to formally declare independence
and the least likely to suffer consequences for it — the empire's navy cannot operate in the
archipelago's channels with the same effectiveness as the Saltborn fleet. The island culture
is fiercely proud, internationally connected, and increasingly irritated by imperial
oversight of a province that barely needs the empire for anything.
Military: The Saltborn Marines — legendary. Thirty-four warships, including six ironclads
that the empire doesn't know have been fitted with Glasswater amplification engines. The
Marines have been running unauthorised wargames in the outer islands for two years.
Magic: Tide magic (Tidebinding) and, uniquely to Glasswater, Volcanic Weave-work —
using the volcanic energy as a Weave amplifier. The outer islands are the only place in Elias
where a mage can, temporarily, exceed what the Weave's normal cost-structure would allow.
The Mage Council knows about this. They have been trying to research it for a decade. Lyrannis
has not invited them.

CRAFT NOTE: Lyrannis should feel like a place that the rest of Elias doesn't fully
understand — cosmopolitan, connected to the wider world beyond the empire, operating by
different social and magical rules. A character from Lyrannis in a room full of mainland
politicians should feel like they're the only adult present. This can be played for comedy
or for tension. It is usually both simultaneously.


PROVINCE 11 — DUSKMORE
The Gray Land | The Contested Province
Economy: ◆◇◇◇◇ Destitute
"Nobody owns Duskmore. This is why everything terrible comes here eventually."
Geography: The borderland between four provinces — Valdris, Thurak, Morvaine, and Solrath
all claim sections of it and none of them maintain it. Rolling wasteland, ruins of three
previous settlements, collapsed roads, poisoned wells from old industrial runoff, and the
Corruption Zones: areas where ancient Weave damage (from a war nobody fully remembers)
manifests as visible reality distortion.
Cities:
CityPopulationCharacterDuskmore Crossing~50,000The gray city. Technically under joint imperial administration; practically ruled by whoever currently has the most guns and the least scruples. The city has had four different governments in eight years. The tavern keepers have learned to repaint the loyalty banners quickly.Ashenveil~18,000The refugee city. Where people who have nowhere else to go end up. The most diverse population in Elias — Solrathi desert-workers, Morvaine factory-refugees, Vethara exiles, army deserters from every province. Originally a military observation post, now a town that exists to observe and report — for whoever pays. Information brokers, spies, assassins, and historians all find Greywatch useful for different reasons.
Political Character: Duskmore is nobody's ally and everybody's problem. The warlords who
run its districts play factions against each other with great skill and no loyalty. The
Corruption Zones are expanding slowly — which nobody is publicly discussing because publicly
discussing them would require acknowledging what caused them.
Military: Warlord militias, army deserters, hired guns from every province. The most heavily
armed civilian population in Elias, with the least institutional capacity to use force
effectively. This is more dangerous than the alternative.
Magic: Corrupted. The Weave here does not behave. A mage who tries to pull a Weave thread
in a Corruption Zone may find it pulling back — hard, in an unexpected direction. Wild magic
erupts randomly, particularly near the old battlefields. Creatures in the deeper Corruption
Zones have been changing for generations.

CRAFT NOTE: Duskmore is your physical manifestation of what political collapse looks like
from the inside. Not dramatic — grinding. The people of Duskmore are not dramatic. They are
tired. They have been tired for a long time. A chapter set entirely in Duskmore — following
a civilian family through an ordinary week — could be the most politically devastating chapter
in the novel, precisely because nothing special is happening. This is just Tuesday in Duskmore.

V. WEAPONS & MILITARY TECHNOLOGY
Elias has reached a technological threshold: the age of reliable firearms has arrived, but
has not yet made conventional soldiers obsolete. A mage is still more powerful than a rifle
squad — until the rifle squad is large enough, fast enough, and has good enough officers.

CRAFT NOTE: The technological gap between provinces is political. Thurak having prototype
bolt-action rifles when the empire is still issuing percussion caps is not just a military
detail — it is a statement about where Thurn's loyalties and ambitions are directing resources.
A character who notices this detail in a military inspection scene understands immediately what
it means. The reader should too.


VI. OPEN QUESTIONS FOR DRAFT V0.2
These are the questions this draft intentionally leaves unanswered. Each one should be decided
before the next draft, because each one shapes the story's architecture.

Who is the protagonist? A single character, or multiple POVs? The world supports either.
Suggestion: One primary protagonist (a character who moves between provinces and factions —
a courier, a spy, an investigator, a mage-bureaucrat from Valdris who knows too much) with
2–3 secondary POVs in key provinces.
What did the Mage Council find in the Aelindra Spire's deep archives? This is the novel's
hidden engine. Recommendation: they discovered that Black-fire Stone extraction is not just
damaging the Weave — it is unravelling it. The Weave is the fabric Elias was woven from.
If enough threads are pulled... the world begins to come undone. The Council has eight months
of data. They have perhaps three years before the process becomes irreversible.
What is beyond Thurak's southern border? Something is there. The Gray Legion has been
fighting it for forty years and telling the empire it is "border skirmishes." It is not
border skirmishes.
What do the Vethara's Eldest trees know? They have been here since before Elias the
Weaver. They may know what he actually was, and what actually happened to him.
What is General Thurn's actual endgame? He is not simply a loyal general watching his
institution fail. He has a plan. What is it?
